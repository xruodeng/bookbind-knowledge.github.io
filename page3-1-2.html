Saddle stitching is a popular method for binding booklets, brochures, magazines, and other publications with a relatively low page count. Here's an overview of the saddle stitching process:

Collating: The first step in saddle stitching is to collate the pages of the publication in the correct order. Pages are typically printed on large sheets of paper, which are then folded into signatures. Each signature contains a group of pages that will be bound together.

Folding: Once the pages are collated into signatures, they are folded along the center fold line. The number of pages per signature depends on factors such as paper thickness and binding equipment capabilities.

Inserting: After folding, the signatures are inserted into each other in the correct order to create the book block. The book block consists of nested signatures, with the pages arranged in sequential order.

Stapling: With the book block assembled, it is then placed on a saddle stitching machine. The machine clamps the book block in place on a saddle-shaped platform, with the spine edge facing upward. A stitching head equipped with wire staples moves along the spine, inserting staples through the fold line and clinching them on the inside of the spine.

Trimming: After stapling, the excess edges of the booklet are trimmed to create clean, uniform edges. This trimming process may be done simultaneously with stapling on some saddle stitching machines, or it may be performed as a separate step using a guillotine cutter.

Quality Control: Once trimmed, the saddle-stitched booklet undergoes quality control checks to ensure that the staples are properly inserted and the pages are aligned correctly. Any defects or inconsistencies are addressed before the booklets are packaged for distribution.

Saddle stitching offers several advantages, including cost-effectiveness, fast production speeds, and the ability to lay flat when opened, making it ideal for publications like magazines, catalogs, and instructional manuals. However, it is best suited for publications with relatively low page counts, as the thickness of the book block is limited by the capacity of the stitching equipment.
